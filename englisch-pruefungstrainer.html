Is Social Media Making Us Lonely?

More and more young people spend hours every day on social media platforms like Instagram, TikTok and Snapchat. While these apps help people stay connected, some experts believe they may also make us feel more lonely.

Dr Sarah Miller, a psychologist from London, explains: “When young people compare their lives to the perfect photos they see online, they often feel that their own lives are boring or not good enough. This can lead to feelings of sadness and loneliness, even when they have hundreds of online friends.”

However, not everyone agrees. A recent study showed that teenagers who use social media to actively communicate — by messaging friends or joining interest groups — reported feeling more connected. The problem seems to be passive use: just scrolling and watching without interacting.

Many schools in Germany and the UK have now introduced “phone-free” breaks during the school day to help students focus and talk to each other face-to-face.

Young People and Climate Change

Climate change is one of the most talked-about topics among young people today. A 2024 survey of 10,000 teenagers across Europe found that 78% are worried about the future of the planet. Many say they feel frustrated because adults are not doing enough.

Sixteen-year-old Lena from Cologne says: “I try to live more sustainably — I cycle instead of taking the car, I eat less meat and I buy second-hand clothes. But it feels like small steps when big companies keep polluting.”

Schools are reacting too. In many German states, climate education is now a mandatory part of the curriculum from Year 5 onwards. Students learn about renewable energy, recycling, and how individual choices affect the environment.

Despite the worries, experts say young people should not lose hope. According to environmental scientist Dr James Park, “The changes that happen now will be decided by the young generation. Their voices in politics and business matter more than ever.”

From School to Work: What Do Employers Want?

Getting your first job after school can feel overwhelming. But what do employers actually look for in young applicants? According to a recent survey of over 500 German companies, the top three things employers want are: good communication skills, reliability, and the ability to work in a team.

Interestingly, having perfect grades was ranked only fifth on the list. “We can teach someone the technical skills of a job,” says HR manager Klaus Berger from a Munich tech company, “but we can’t easily teach someone to be punctual, honest or to work well with others.”

For young people preparing for the job market, experts recommend doing internships (“Praktikum”) as early as possible. An internship gives you real experience and shows employers that you are motivated. Many companies in Germany even offer apprenticeships (“Ausbildung”) which combine work and school.

The job market is changing fast. Skills like digital literacy, creativity and problem-solving are becoming more and more important — especially as artificial intelligence starts to take over routine tasks.
