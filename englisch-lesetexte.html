My Life as a Teenager

A short story about growing up in a small place — with all the highs and lows.

Living in a small neighbourhood has many advantages. Everyone knows each other, and people support each other when something happens — a broken pipe, a sick neighbour, a lost cat. Of course, there are also some disadvantages. Sometimes I want more privacy, and on weekends there isn’t much to do here.

I’m 15 years old, and this year I have learned a lot about responsibility. My parents both work full-time, so I have to take care of my little brother after school. At first, I used to complain about it — I thought it was unfair. But now I understand that it’s just part of growing up, and honestly, I like spending time with him.

School is going well, but I want to improve my English before the next exam. So I read books in English every evening, even when I’m tired. It isn’t always convenient, but I know it will give me better opportunities in the future — for university, for a job, for travelling.

My family can’t always afford expensive holidays abroad, but we still have great experiences together. Last summer we went camping in the mountains. The weather was terrible — it rained for two days — but it was one of the best weeks of my life.

One thing I try to do at school is avoid people with bad behaviour. There are always some students who make fun of others or start drama for no reason. I prefer to stay with my real friends — people I can trust.

Growing Up in a Digital World

A look at the pressures and possibilities that shape today’s teenagers.

Being a teenager today is not easy. We have to cope with so many things at once — school, social media, family expectations, and the constant feeling that we have to be perfect all the time. The current generation faces challenges our parents never had to deal with.

One major issue that has a huge impact on young people is social media. Of course, it has some good sides — it helps us stay in touch with friends and encourages creativity. But there are also serious drawbacks. Many teenagers compare themselves to influencers and feel bad about their own lives. We must be aware of how much time we spend online and how it makes us feel afterwards.

I think parents and teachers don’t always take into account how much pressure we are under. A significant part of our stress comes from school: too much homework, too many tests, and not enough time to just be young.

At the same time, we cannot just rely on adults to solve everything for us. We also have to contribute to the solution — by being honest about how we feel, asking for help when we need it, and supporting each other instead of competing.

C1 LEVEL 🏠 EVERYDAY LIFE ~210 words 12 vocab

Modern Life: Pressures and Possibilities

A more academic look at the realities of growing up today.

Modern life encompasses many challenges that previous generations did not face. From digital overload to climate anxiety, today’s youth must navigate a world full of complexity. The long-term implications of growing up in this environment are still not fully understood.

Researchers have begun to scrutinize the link between social media use and mental health, and the early results are concerning. Excessive screen time can exacerbate existing anxiety and undermine a young person’s self-esteem — especially when comparison with others becomes constant. These issues are particularly prevalent in urban areas, where the social comparison rarely stops.

Yet, technology also has inherent benefits. It can facilitate learning, connect us across continents, and give a voice to those who would otherwise be silent. To substantiate any claim about whether technology is “good” or “bad”, one must look at how it is used, not just whether it is used.

If we want to mitigate the negative effects, the approach is surprisingly simple: set clear boundaries, take real breaks, and elaborate on your feelings with people you trust. Don’t let your sense of self diminish just because someone else’s online life looks “perfect” — most of the time, it isn’t.

Our Planet, Our Responsibility

A teenager’s honest take on small actions that can change the bigger picture.

Last year I started thinking more about climate change and how it affects our future. It scares me sometimes — global warming is making summers hotter, animals are losing their homes, and air pollution in big cities is getting worse every year.

My friends and I decided to make small changes. We don’t always need to fly on holidays — flying massively increases our carbon footprint. Now we travel by train when we can. It’s slower, but the views are amazing.

At home, my family started to recycle everything we can: paper, plastic, glass. We also try to reduce how much we waste — both food and energy. Mum even bought reusable shopping bags. It took some getting used to, but now it feels normal.

I’m especially worried about endangered animals. So many species are disappearing because we damage their habitats for farms and cities. That’s why I support organizations that protect rainforests and oceans.

Of course, big change has to come from governments and companies. Switching to renewable energy like solar and wind is the most important step. But I still believe small actions add up. If everyone tries to live a more sustainable life, we can make a real difference.

B2 LEVEL 🌍 ENVIRONMENT ~210 words 12 vocab

The Hidden Cost of Modern Life

Behind every product, every flight, every meal — a planet quietly paying the bill.

We rarely stop to think about it, but our daily consumption has a serious environmental cost. Every car, every flight, every burger contributes to a system that is slowly destroying our planet.

The numbers are alarming. Industries around the world still rely heavily on fossil fuels — coal, oil and gas — which emit huge amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat and accelerate climate change. As a result, we see more extreme weather: longer droughts, stronger storms, melting glaciers.

Equally serious is the loss of biodiversity. Many animals are now close to extinction because we keep damaging their habitats. Deforestation in tropical regions is one of the worst offenders — every minute, an area of rainforest the size of a football field disappears. Entire ecosystems collapse when their key species vanish.

If we continue at this pace, we will rapidly deplete the natural resources our planet provides — fresh water, healthy soil, clean air. Governments must act faster, but consumers also have power. By choosing local food, repairing instead of throwing away, and pressuring companies to cut their emissions, we can help preserve what remains. The clock is ticking.

Climate Justice and the Road Ahead

Why decarbonization is no longer a choice — and what justice demands of our generation.

The climate crisis has reached a stage at which inaction is no longer defensible. Scientists warn that some of the most severe consequences — melting ice caps, ecosystem collapse, mass extinctions — are now effectively irreversible. The question is no longer whether change is needed, but how quickly we can deliver it.

At the centre of the debate stands decarbonization. Most major economies have committed to achieving carbon neutrality by the middle of the century, yet very few are on track. To phase out coal and oil while building reliable alternatives requires unprecedented coordination between governments, businesses, and citizens.

A growing number of activists advocate for a broader vision: the circular economy. Rather than the current linear model of “take, make, throw away”, a circular system focuses on reuse and repair, sharply reducing the ecological footprint of everyday life.

Yet technology alone will not be enough. Urbanization, overconsumption and inequality continue to exacerbate the degradation of natural systems. Building climate resilience in the most vulnerable communities — those least responsible for emissions — is now a moral imperative.

This is where the principle of intergenerational justice becomes central. We must not pass on a damaged planet to children who never had a vote in how we chose to offset — or refused to offset — our own pollution.
